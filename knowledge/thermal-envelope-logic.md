# Thermal Envelope Philosophy

The exterior panel system should be understood as part of the building enclosure.

Building enclosure logic:

* The enclosure separates conditioned interior space from exterior climate.
* Exterior systems must manage heat, air, vapor, bulk water, and drying behavior.
* A panel can contribute to these control functions, but the full assembly determines performance.

Conditioned vs exterior environment:

* The interior side may be conditioned by heating, cooling, and humidity control.
* The exterior side is exposed to rain, wind, solar radiation, temperature swings, and moisture.
* The thermal envelope must reduce uncontrolled heat transfer between these environments.

Integrated exterior thermal workflow:

* Exterior finish, metal layer, PU insulation core, backing layer, air gap, accessories, and fixing sequence should be coordinated.
* Thermal control is not only material selection; it is also installation continuity.
* Gaps, poorly aligned joints, unplanned penetrations, and discontinuous insulation can weaken envelope behavior.

Thermal continuity:

* Continuous insulation logic aims to maintain insulation across broad wall areas.
* Continuity must be checked at joints, corners, openings, trims, fixings, and substrate transitions.

# Thermal Control Layer

The thermal control layer reduces heat flow through the enclosure.

Continuous insulation:

* Continuous insulation reduces heat transfer across the main wall field.
* A PU core can contribute to this thermal layer within the panel body.
* The thermal control layer should remain as uninterrupted as possible.

Heat flow resistance:

* Insulation slows conductive heat flow.
* Metal layers and fasteners can conduct heat more readily than insulation.
* Complete wall thermal behavior depends on all components, not only the insulated panel area.

Thermal continuity:

* Panel-to-panel joints must be aligned and closed properly.
* Corner, edge, and opening details should avoid unnecessary insulation breaks.
* Installation errors can create thermal weak points.

Minimizing thermal bridges:

* Limit unnecessary metal continuity through the insulation layer where design allows.
* Coordinate fixings, trims, and frame conditions with thermal control intent.
* Review transitions and penetrations as thermal bridge risk points.

# PU Insulation Core Logic

The PU insulation core is the primary insulation component within the panel.

Insulation continuity:

* The core provides insulation across the panel field.
* Continuity depends on panel alignment, joint treatment, edge closure, and trim sequencing.
* Damaged or poorly seated panels can reduce thermal consistency.

Integrated thermal workflow:

* Insulation is installed as part of the exterior panel workflow.
* This can reduce separate site steps compared with independent insulation and finish layers.
* Integrated insulation still requires correct installation and complete envelope detailing.

Lightweight thermal envelope:

* PU core supports a lighter insulated panel structure.
* Lightweight panels can improve handling and installation repeatability.
* Exact weight and thermal performance require product-specific data.

Reduced heat transfer:

* The core helps reduce heat flow through panel field areas.
* Thermal performance cannot be assumed at joints, fixings, frames, openings, or transitions without assembly review.

# Thermal Bridging

Thermal bridges are paths where heat bypasses or weakens the insulation layer.

Common thermal bridge locations:

* metal frames
* screws and fasteners
* panel joints
* corners
* window and door openings
* slab or structural interfaces
* trim and accessory transitions
* damaged or missing insulation zones

Why discontinuous insulation causes problems:

* Heat can move faster through conductive or uninsulated paths.
* Surface temperatures may become uneven.
* Interior comfort and energy performance may be affected.
* Cold surfaces may increase condensation risk in some climates.

Condensation risk:

* Thermal bridges can create colder interior-side surfaces.
* If surface temperature drops below dew point, condensation can occur.
* Condensation risk depends on temperature, humidity, vapor movement, air leakage, and assembly design.

Heat loss pathways:

* Heat can bypass the panel field through continuous metal elements.
* Poorly detailed joints can reduce insulation continuity.
* Frame and fixing layout should be reviewed as part of envelope design.

# Moisture & Condensation Logic

Moisture behavior must be managed together with thermal behavior.

Condensation formation:

* Condensation occurs when moisture-laden air reaches a surface below dew point.
* This can happen on interior surfaces, inside cavities, or within assemblies depending on temperature and vapor conditions.
* Thermal bridges and air leakage can increase condensation risk.

Trapped moisture risks:

* Moisture trapped behind panels or inside cavities can reduce durability.
* Blocked drainage or ventilation paths can slow drying.
* Wet insulation or damp backing conditions should be avoided.

Vapor behavior:

* Vapor moves by diffusion through materials and by air leakage through gaps.
* Vapor control depends on full wall assembly design, not one panel layer alone.
* Aluminum foil backing may affect vapor behavior, but project-specific wall buildup determines the result.

Ventilation importance:

* Ventilation paths can help release incidental moisture where designed.
* Air gaps must remain clear to support drying.
* Ventilation logic must coordinate with drainage and waterproof sequencing.

Drying pathways:

* outward drying through ventilated cavity where designed
* downward drainage of incidental water
* controlled air circulation behind cladding
* moisture release through compatible wall assembly layers

# Rainscreen & Ventilation Logic

Rainscreen logic helps manage water and drying behind the exterior cladding.

Ventilated cavity:

* A ventilated cavity is a space behind the cladding that can support air movement and drying.
* It must be intentionally designed and kept open.
* Do not claim rainscreen behavior unless the assembly includes a designed cavity and ventilation path.

Drainage plane:

* A drainage plane provides a path for incidental water to move downward and out.
* The drainage plane must connect to lower exits or weep paths.
* Trim and starter conditions must not block drainage.

Breathing wall logic:

* Breathing wall behavior depends on coordinated layers, not the panel alone.
* Catalog diagrams may show air circulation gaps or breathing-wall concepts.
* These concepts require assembly-level design to function correctly.

Moisture release:

* Air circulation can help remove moisture from behind cladding.
* Drainage cavities can release bulk water that passes the outer layer.
* Top and bottom openings must be coordinated with pest protection, fire requirements, and local code.

Facade drying behavior:

* exterior surface sheds bulk rain
* trims and overlaps reduce direct entry
* cavity drains incidental water
* ventilation helps drying where designed

# Air & Vapor Logic

Air and vapor movement affect thermal and moisture performance.

Uncontrolled air leakage:

* Air leakage can carry moisture into the wall assembly.
* Air leakage can increase heat loss and condensation risk.
* Panel joints and penetrations should be coordinated with the air-control strategy of the full wall.

Vapor movement:

* Vapor diffusion moves through materials based on vapor pressure difference.
* Vapor control depends on climate, interior humidity, materials, and wall buildup.
* Foil backing may resist vapor movement, but its effect depends on assembly continuity and placement.

Enclosure continuity:

* The four control layers are generally understood as water, air, vapor, and thermal control.
* These layers should be continuous across field areas, openings, corners, and transitions.
* Exterior panels can support some control functions but do not replace complete enclosure design.

Controlled moisture behavior:

* manage bulk water through drainage and overlap
* manage air leakage through compatible wall assembly detailing
* manage vapor through material selection and placement
* manage drying through ventilation or vapor-open pathways where designed

# Layer Integration Logic

The exterior enclosure works when layers and installation logic support each other.

Metal skin:

* provides exposed weather-facing surface
* supports facade durability and surface protection
* must be coordinated with drainage and edge protection

Insulation core:

* provides thermal resistance across panel field areas
* supports integrated insulation workflow
* requires continuity at joints and transitions

Foil backing:

* protects rear side of the insulation core
* may influence vapor or reflective behavior depending on assembly
* must not be treated as a complete moisture strategy by itself

Air cavity:

* supports drainage and drying when intentionally designed
* requires clear airflow and drainage exits
* must not be blocked by sealant, foam, debris, or incorrect installation

Trims:

* organize starts, joints, corners, openings, and edges
* reduce exposed gaps
* support panel alignment and water-control sequencing
* help maintain continuity across facade transitions

Integrated system logic:

* surface sheds water
* insulation reduces heat flow
* backing protects panel core
* cavity drains and dries where designed
* trims close transitions
* installation sequence preserves continuity

# Buildability & Thermal Logic

Thermal envelope performance depends on buildable installation.

Integrated installation:

* finish and insulation are installed together as a panel system
* accessories define transitions before panels are fully fixed
* dry fixing supports repeatable construction workflow

Insulation continuity during installation:

* panel edges should align cleanly
* joints should be controlled
* openings and corners should be planned before cutting and fixing
* damaged insulation zones should be avoided or corrected

Simplified exterior execution:

* fewer separate layers reduce coordination burden
* repeatable panel placement supports consistent thermal coverage
* trim sequencing supports closure and inspection

Scalable thermal envelope construction:

* the same thermal-envelope logic can repeat across wall areas
* inspection points become predictable: joints, edges, corners, openings, fixings, drainage, and ventilation paths
* buildability improves when crews can install thermal and facade layers in a clear sequence

# Common Misunderstandings

## Waterproof vs Vapor Control

Misunderstanding:

* Waterproofing and vapor control are treated as the same thing.

Engineering correction:

* Waterproofing manages bulk water.
* Vapor control manages water vapor movement.
* A system may shed rain well but still require vapor and condensation review.

## Decorative Panels Are Only Aesthetic

Misunderstanding:

* Exterior panels are viewed only as facade decoration.

Engineering correction:

* The system can contribute to finish, insulation, water management, edge closure, and installation workflow.
* Appearance is one part of a broader enclosure system.

## Thermal Bridging Is Ignored

Misunderstanding:

* Continuous panel insulation eliminates all thermal weak points.

Engineering correction:

* Thermal bridges can occur at fasteners, frames, joints, corners, openings, and transitions.
* Complete wall assembly review is required.

## Rainscreen Ventilation Is Automatic

Misunderstanding:

* Any exterior panel installation automatically creates a functioning rainscreen.

Engineering correction:

* Rainscreen behavior requires a designed cavity, drainage plane, ventilation path, and compatible detailing.
* Blocked cavities or missing exits prevent proper drying and drainage.

## Foil Backing Solves Moisture Control Alone

Misunderstanding:

* Aluminum foil backing alone controls all moisture behavior.

Engineering correction:

* Moisture control depends on full assembly design: drainage, air leakage, vapor movement, cavity drying, and substrate condition.
* Foil backing is one layer within the larger system.
