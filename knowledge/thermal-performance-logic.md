# Thermal Performance Logic

This document defines thermal-performance reasoning for Struvia exterior systems.

Reference base:

* uploaded technical specification Excel file
* `knowledge/thermal-envelope-logic.md`
* `knowledge/product-structure.md`

Important interpretation:

* Thermal values are references for engineering reasoning.
* Complete wall thermal performance depends on the full assembly, not the panel field alone.
* Do not invent energy ratings, climate-zone compliance, or whole-building performance claims.

# Insulation Continuity

Thermal performance depends on continuity.

Panel field behavior:

* the PU core provides insulation across the panel body
* repeated panel width can support regular thermal coverage
* factory-integrated insulation can reduce separate insulation installation steps

Continuity risk points:

* panel joints
* corners
* cut edges
* window and door openings
* screw lines
* metal trims
* substrate transitions
* damaged core areas

Buildability relationship:

* panels must seat correctly before fixing
* trims should close edges without creating unnecessary gaps
* insulation continuity should be inspected after cutting and around openings
* repeated installation sequence improves thermal consistency

---

# Heat Transfer Control

Heat moves through the exterior wall by conduction, convection, and radiation.

Conductive control:

* PU foam reduces conductive heat flow in panel field areas
* metal skins and fasteners conduct heat more readily than insulation
* thermal bridges must be considered at connection points

Convective control:

* air leakage can carry heat and moisture
* panel joints alone should not be treated as the building air barrier unless designed and tested as such
* the wall assembly needs a continuous air-control strategy

Radiant and surface behavior:

* exterior metal surfaces respond to solar exposure and ambient temperature
* color, coating, ventilation, and wall buildup can affect surface temperature behavior
* thermal comfort claims require complete assembly and climate analysis

# R-Value Implications

The R-value reference supports insulation discussion, but it must be used carefully.

Appropriate use:

* explain resistance to heat flow
* compare insulation logic at a material or panel-reference level
* support thermal-envelope continuity content

Inappropriate use:

* claiming whole-wall performance without assembly calculation
* ignoring metal fasteners and trims
* converting directly into building energy savings
* using the value as proof of code compliance without local review
