# Export Workflow Philosophy

Export workflow protects panel geometry, surface finish, interlocking edges, and package stability from factory handoff to overseas unloading.

Core logic:

* protect panels before container loading
* load by sequence and geometry
* reduce friction between packages
* prevent edge and corner deformation
* control moisture exposure
* optimize container use without damaging product
* make unloading predictable for the receiving team

# Container Geometry Logic

Container loading must consider panel length, package shape, pallet dimensions, and door clearance.

Reference article logic:

* 2.9 m panel packaging is described as suitable for palletized container loading.
* 5.8 m panel packaging is described as long-panel loading that requires stronger sequence control.
* Container space must be checked before and during loading.

Dimension PDF logic:

* 20 ft container compatible lengths: 2.9 m or 5.8 m per piece.
* 40 ft high-cube container compatible lengths: 2.9 m, 3.8 m, or 5.8 m per piece.
* 20 ft loading reference: about 1,500 m2.
* 40 ft high-cube loading reference: about 3,500 m2.
* Loading references are approximate and must be verified against packaging method, pallet use, accessories, and order mix.

Engineering considerations:

* long panels have higher bending and edge-damage risk
* package length affects forklift movement and loading angle
* door clearance must be confirmed before final closure
* load sequence should avoid trapping unstable packages near the door

# Loading Sequencing

Loading sequence should be planned before packages enter the container.

Recommended logic:

1. inspect container condition
2. confirm package labels and destination
3. load stable base packages first
4. maintain package alignment
5. separate packages where friction or compression risk exists
6. secure long or vulnerable panel stacks
7. remove leftover packaging debris
8. confirm final door closure

Article-derived evidence:

* the article shows container cleaning after loading
* final door closure is part of the workflow
* package labels are used after boxing
* palletized and long-panel methods are treated differently

# Long Panel Transport Risk

Long panels require additional handling control.

Risk areas:

* bending under unsupported length
* edge deformation during forklift handling
* abrasion between long packages
* corner damage near container entry
* unloading difficulty at destination

Control logic:

* use proper support points
* avoid dragging packages
* maintain straight package alignment
* reinforce vulnerable edges where required
* plan unloading access before loading sequence is finalized

Length compatibility:

* 2.9 m lengths generally simplify handling compared with longer bundles.
* 3.8 m lengths are referenced for 40 ft high-cube planning.
* 5.8 m lengths require stronger support and deformation control.

# Weight and Volume Planning

The dimension PDF gives reference values per 1000 m2.

Weight:

* 16 mm: 3000 kg
* 20 mm: 3300 kg

Volume:

* 16 mm: 18 m3
* 20 mm: 20 m3

Export planning use:

* estimate total shipment weight by thickness and area
* estimate package volume before container planning
* coordinate panel thickness with loading quantity and handling method
* evaluate weight and volume together, not separately

Important caution:

* these values are planning references, not fixed container capacity guarantees
* actual shipment planning should include cartons, pallets, accessories, protective materials, and container condition

# Unloading Considerations

Export workflow should consider the receiving side.

Unloading logic:

* labels should remain visible where possible
* packages should be arranged so first-access items can be removed safely
* long packages should not require unsafe bending or twisting during removal
* pallets should be stable enough for forklift handling
* moisture-damaged or shifted packages should be inspected before site distribution

# Deformation Prevention

Deformation prevention protects installation performance.

Critical areas:

* interlocking edges
* cut ends
* visible surface face
* corners
* long-panel spans
* stacked package contact points

Methods:

* carton protection
* pallet sequencing
* film wrapping
* plank or edge reinforcement
* controlled stacking direction
* container separation where needed

# Handling & Destination Protection Logic

Reference source:

* uploaded handling, storage, cutting, and construction precautions document

Export interpretation:

* export protection must preserve panel geometry, coating surface, interlocking edges, and cut-edge condition until installation
* long panels remain vulnerable after delivery; receiving-side handling and storage should be treated as part of export workflow guidance
* deformation risk increases when support is concentrated at only one or two points

Transportation handling logic:

* panels longer than 3 m should be handled with distributed support, including both ends and the center
* worker movement should be coordinated so the panel is not twisted or allowed to sag
* lifting tools such as slings or lifting rods can reduce direct pressure on coated surfaces
* surface grooves, scratches, edge impact, and center sag should be treated as preventable transport damage modes

Receiving-side storage guidance:

* store panels in a ventilated and dry warehouse where possible
* use a flat, firm site for temporary storage
* when outdoor storage is unavoidable, add moisture-proof and waterproof covering
* use square timber or pallet support to keep panels away from standing water
* keep support spacing close enough to prevent deformation from panel self-weight
* avoid open-air rain exposure and soaking

Export workflow implication:

* shipment handoff should include handling and storage precautions, not only packing quantity
* destination teams should understand long-panel lifting, support spacing, waterproof covering, and surface protection before unloading begins
