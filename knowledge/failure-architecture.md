# Failure Architecture

Failure architecture explains how exterior panel workflows break down. It connects cause, physical condition, risk, inspection, and correction.

## Failure Chain Format

Use this structure in Technical Center pages:

1. Trigger: what action or condition starts the problem.
2. Physical condition: what changes in the assembly.
3. Risk behavior: how water, heat, load, corrosion, or deformation develops.
4. Inspection point: where the problem should be caught.
5. Correction: what must be fixed before work continues.
6. Prevention: what sequencing rule prevents recurrence.

## Common Failure Chains

### Trapped Moisture

Trigger: drainage outlet is blocked by sealant, foam, debris, or trim placement.

Physical condition: water enters a detail but cannot leave.

Risk behavior: moisture remains behind trim or at lower edges.

Inspection point: lower outlet, sill release, starter trim, or cavity exit.

Correction: reopen the drainage path and clean debris.

Prevention: inspect drainage before final closure.

### Wrong Overlap

Trigger: trim, flashing, or panel edge is installed in reverse lap direction.

Physical condition: water is directed behind the cladding.

Risk behavior: hidden leakage, wet cut edges, or moisture retention.

Inspection point: head flashing, overlap joint, corner trim, base termination.

Correction: reset the lap direction before repeated installation.

Prevention: verify overlap direction before fixing and sealing.

### Blocked Drainage

Trigger: outlet path is sealed or filled during installation.

Physical condition: lower release route is closed.

Risk behavior: hidden ponding and wet lower edge.

Inspection point: base trim, starter trim, sill outlet, drainage hole.

Correction: remove obstruction and confirm the designed outlet.

Prevention: treat drainage gaps as control features, not cosmetic defects.

### Thermal Bridge

Trigger: joint, fastener, trim, or substrate creates a conductive path.

Physical condition: insulation layer is interrupted or bypassed.

Risk behavior: localized heat transfer and possible cold-surface risk.

Inspection point: cut edges, fasteners, trims, openings, transitions.

Correction: review detail against project thermal design and protect continuity where required.

Prevention: do not infer whole-wall performance from panel data alone.

### Unsupported Edge

Trigger: panel edge, corner, or base lacks proper support or restraint.

Physical condition: edge deflects, opens, rattles, or becomes impact-sensitive.

Risk behavior: joint gaps, trim movement, surface distortion, or exposed core.

Inspection point: corner trim, starter trim, interlock, substrate line.

Correction: restore support, seating, or fixing before closure.

Prevention: check substrate and support before panel installation.

### Cutting Corrosion

Trigger: metal filings remain on coated surfaces or cut edges.

Physical condition: debris is exposed to moisture.

Risk behavior: staining, surface damage, or corrosion initiation.

Inspection point: lower trims, cut zones, corners, openings, field cuts.

Correction: remove filings and clean with appropriate non-damaging methods.

Prevention: clean immediately after cutting and before rain exposure.
