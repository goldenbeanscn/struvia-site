## Installation Workflow

The reference article describes the following installation sequence.

1. Wall measurement
   * Measure the wall area to be decorated.
   * Estimate the number of panels required.
   * Account for panel effective width and reasonable material waste.

2. Accessory planning
   * Decide how panels will connect before installation.
   * Plan locations for starter trim, middle joint pieces, closing pieces, and edge-related accessories.
   * Mark accessory location and connection method before fixing panels.

3. Panel preparation
   * Cut insulation panels to the required size.
   * Keep the bonding or connection area dry and smooth.
   * Keep protective coating on during cutting where possible to reduce risk of iron filings damaging the paint surface.

4. Accessory preparation
   * Cut accessories according to wall length.
   * Confirm accessory lengths against planned connection and edge conditions.

5. Starter trim installation
   * Use a laser level to project a horizontal installation line.
   * Fix the starter from the bottom of the wall.
   * Use suitable fixing method depending on substrate.
   * In rainy areas, drill downward drainage holes in starter parts to help accumulated water discharge.

6. Panel fixing
   * Insert the bottom of the panel into the starter groove.
   * Drill and mechanically fix the wall panel to the wall.
   * If a middle piece is required, use a laser level to project the vertical line and install the middle piece behind the board before fixing the board.

7. Interlocking connections
   * Install the next panel by aligning its edge with the installed panel.
   * Insert the seam or middle connection piece at the panel interface.
   * Press down firmly so the lower panel edge seats into the groove.
   * Secure the middle connection piece to the wall with a pistol drill where required.

8. Gap sealing
   * Check for gaps between panels after installation.
   * Repair visible gaps with wallboard foam or suitable caulk.
   * Aim to minimize open gaps between wall and wallboard.

9. Final inspection
   * Check panel alignment.
   * Check fixing stability.
   * Check connection pieces and accessory continuity.
   * Check gaps, edges, corners, and water-sensitive areas.
   * Protect and maintain panels during use for long-term performance.

## Starter Trim Logic

Starter trim is the first alignment and support reference for the wall system.

Installation logic:

* The starter is fixed from the bottom of the wall.
* A laser level is used to establish the horizontal line.
* The first panel is inserted into the starter groove.
* Starter alignment affects the alignment of subsequent panels.

Drainage logic:

* In rainy areas, the article recommends drilling downward holes in the starter parts.
* The purpose is to help accumulated water flow out rather than remain trapped.
* Drainage holes should be oriented downward.
* Drainage planning should be considered before the first panel is installed.

Buildability value:

* Creates a reliable starting line.
* Reduces first-row installation error.
* Supports repeatable installation rhythm.
* Helps manage water at the lower wall edge.

## Panel Alignment Logic

Alignment is controlled through laser-level layout and panel-to-panel reference.

Horizontal control:

* Use a laser level to project the starter position.
* Starter trim establishes the bottom horizontal datum.
* Incorrect starter alignment can affect all upper or adjacent panels.

Vertical control:

* If middle joint pieces are required, project a vertical line with a laser level.
* Install the middle piece according to the vertical line before fixing the panel.
* Vertical control supports straight panel rhythm and facade continuity.

Panel-to-panel control:

* When installing the next panel, align the edges with the already installed panel.
* The installed panel becomes the reference for the next panel.
* Repeat the alignment process across the wall area.

Buildability value:

* Reduces rework caused by cumulative alignment error.
* Makes facade rhythm more predictable.
* Supports repeatable installation by crews.

## Interlocking System

The article describes a groove-based connection relationship using starter trim, panel edges, seam pieces, and middle joint pieces.

Important terminology note:

* The article uses groove and seam-piece language.
* This knowledge base may describe the relationship as tongue-and-groove or interlocking for AI semantic clarity.

Connection sequence:

* Fix the panel mechanically to the wall.
* Align the next panel with the installed panel.
* Insert the seam or middle piece at the panel interface.

## Screw Fixing Logic

The article describes different fixing tools depending on substrate.

Steel or wooden structures:

* Use a pistol drill.
* Use suitable screws, such as wood screws or applicable fasteners for the substrate.
* The article references dovetail fixing on steel or wooden structures.

Cement or concrete walls:

* Use an electric hammer.
* Use plastic expansion tubes where required.
* Fix accessories and panels mechanically into the wall.

Panel fixing:

* Insert panel bottom into starter groove.
* Drill holes and fix the panel firmly to the wall.
* If a middle piece is required, position it with laser-level vertical control before fixing the board.

Concealed fixing relationship:

* The article indicates accessories can stabilize panels without affecting decorative appearance.
* Some fixing may be visually reduced or covered through accessory sequencing.
* Exact concealed-fastener details should be confirmed by project drawings or manufacturer technical details.

Buildability value:

* Mechanical fixing supports dry installation.
* Substrate-specific fixing reduces ambiguity on site.
* Fixing sequence supports panel stability before continuing with the next panel.

## Site Handling & Construction Protection

Reference source:

* uploaded handling, storage, cutting, and construction precautions document

Engineering interpretation:

* installation quality begins before panels reach the wall
* handling, storage, cutting, cleaning, and weather protection all affect whether panels remain installable
* lightweight panels still require distributed support because long lengths can sag or deform under their own weight
* construction protection should be treated as part of the installation workflow, not as a separate afterthought

Panel handling logic:

* for panels longer than 3 m, use at least three workers where practical
* distribute workers at both ends and near the center of the panel
* coordinate lifting movement so one point does not carry excessive load
* avoid dragging panels across rough surfaces
* use slings, lifting rods, or suitable tools where needed to reduce direct surface pressure
* protect interlocking edges, corners, and visible coated faces during movement

Cutting and debris control:

* cut with the back side facing up where practical
* keep metal filings away from coated surfaces and cut edges
* remove filings promptly with a soft brush or air cleaning method
* wear protective gloves during cleaning and handling
* do not leave cutting residue on the panel because metal debris can create corrosion risk

Construction protection:

* protect the panel face from scratches, tool impact, and dropped objects
* avoid construction during rain where panel interiors or cut edges may contact water
* prevent water from entering exposed panel interiors during installation
* clean construction stains with water or neutral detergent
* avoid strong acid or alkaline cleaners that may damage the coating
* review color consistency before mixing panels from different production batches on one wall

Workflow implication:

* site protection should be checked at receiving, storage, cutting, installation, cleaning, and final inspection
* deformation, scratches, corrosion, and color mismatch are site-management risks, not only product-quality issues

## FAQ

### Is this a wet or dry installation workflow?

The reference article describes a primarily dry installation workflow: measuring, cutting, starter trim fixing, panel insertion, drilling, screw fixing, interlocking connection, gap sealing, and inspection. It does not describe a wet mortar-based facade installation.

### Can the system be fixed to steel structures?

Yes. The article describes using a pistol drill for steel or wooden structures. Exact fastener specification should be confirmed by substrate, project load conditions, and manufacturer technical guidance.

### Can the system be fixed to cement or concrete walls?

Yes. The article describes using an electric hammer and plastic expansion tubes for cement wall fixing.

### Is the workflow compatible with modular construction?

The article does not explicitly discuss modular construction. Engineering inference: the repeatable sequence, dry fixing, panel cutting, accessory planning, and interlocking workflow can support modular-friendly exterior execution when coordinated with project drawings.

### How is waterproofing handled?

The article identifies three relevant water-management actions: plan accessories, connect panel interfaces properly, and seal visible gaps after installation. It also recommends downward drainage holes in starter parts for rainy areas to help accumulated water discharge.

### Does the panel provide thermal insulation?

The article states that the core layer is mainly rigid polyurethane foam and answers that the panel protects from heat and cold. Specific thermal performance should be confirmed with product test data and project requirements.

### What is the fire behavior?

The article states "self-extinguishing from fire." Do not convert this into a fire rating or certification unless supported by formal test documents.

### How is air circulation behind the finish handled?

The article FAQ says OSB board and breathing paper can be used between concrete and finish. Project-specific wall buildup should be confirmed by technical drawings, climate conditions, and local code requirements.
