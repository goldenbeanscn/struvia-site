Waterproof Logic

Water management documentation.

Exterior water control is organized through surface shedding, shingle-style overlap, interlocking continuity, trims, drainage exits, flashing coordination, and drying paths where designed.

Primary Sources

waterproof-logic.md, thermal-envelope-logic.md, construction-protection-logic.md

Technical Overview

The waterproof framework reduces intrusion risk by giving water a predictable path: down, out, and away from vulnerable transitions. It does not replace project flashing, wall assembly design, or local waterproofing requirements.

01 / Overlap

Gravity direction

Upper elements should shed over lower elements to avoid reverse-lap water entry.

02 / Drainage

Exit path logic

If water can enter a detail, it needs a designed route to leave.

03 / Trims

Transition control

Starter, corner, connecting, and opening trims organize water-sensitive edges.

04 / Drying

Ventilation caution

Rainscreen or breathing-wall behavior requires designed cavity and ventilation paths.

Practical Workflow

Water-control sequence

Waterproof buildability comes from sequencing accessories and inspection before final closure.

01

Identify risk zones

Openings, corners, lower edges, penetrations, and exposed cuts.

02

Plan overlaps

Confirm shingle direction before fixing trims and panels.

03

Keep exits open

Do not block intended drainage paths with sealant, foam, or debris.

04

Inspect after closure

Check gaps, reverse laps, drainage holes, and flashing relationships.

Failure & Checkpoint Architecture

Engineering documentation should describe how workflows break, where risk concentrates, and what must be checked before the next step proceeds.

Moisture Caution

Water control depends on overlap, drainage exits, flashing, drying paths, and sequencing. Do not block intended exits.

Thermal Discontinuity Risk

Joints, fasteners, trims, openings, cuts, and damaged cores can interrupt insulation continuity.

Cutting Corrosion Alert

Metal filings must be removed from coated surfaces and cut edges before moisture exposure.

Unsupported Storage

Wide pallet spacing or uneven supports can deform long panels under their own weight.

Improper Overlap

Reverse-lap sequencing can direct water behind the cladding instead of outward.

Checkpoint Rule

Verify the control point before moving to the next stage: seat, fix, close, drain, clean, inspect.

Waterproof Validation Workflow

This validation layer turns waterproof logic into inspection action across openings, base drainage, overlaps, trims, and cleaning work.

Failure Chain

Wrong overlap or blocked drainage -> water moves behind the panel or cannot exit -> moisture is trapped at openings, lower trims, or cut edges -> inspect and correct before closure.

Inspection Logic

Verify overlap direction, trim continuity, drainage outlets, flashing closure, cleaning after cutting, and moisture-risk zones.

Risk Visualization

Use warning zones for openings and base outlets, moisture-trap markers for sill/base pockets, and water arrows for intended down-and-out movement.

Before Install

Map water-sensitive zones: openings, corners, lower edge, overlaps, penetrations, and exposed cuts.

During Install

Check shingle direction, drainage path, trim closure, and debris removal before sealant or repeated panel work.

After Install

Inspect for reverse laps, blocked outlets, trapped debris, exposed core, and unrecorded closure details.

Correction Workflow

Stop the sequence, reopen blocked exits, reset overlap direction, clean filings, document the correction, and then continue.

FAQ

Is the panel absolutely waterproof?

No. The system should be described as water-management logic requiring correct detailing.

What is the biggest site risk?

Reverse overlap, blocked drainage, poor flashing, exposed gaps, and trapped moisture.

Are drainage holes always needed?

They are used where recommended by project or installation guidance, especially in rainy conditions.

Is rainscreen behavior automatic?

No. It requires a designed cavity, ventilation path, and drainage plane.
